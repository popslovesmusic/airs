# Manual Section 07: Modal Symmetry Paradigm (MSP) Foundational Specification

## 7.0 Preamble

This document defines the architecture, structure, and function of the **Modal Symmetry Paradigm (MSP)**, which is the first structured layer emerging from the relational Substrate. MSP is the geometric and modal formatting layer of the framework, providing the initial structure capable of supporting meaning.

It explains: what MSP is, why it exists, how it functions, what it makes possible, and how it connects the Substrate to MSC and MBC.

## 7.1 Role in the Ontology Stack

MSP occupies the second conceptual layer in the monistic hierarchy, acting as the bridge from raw potential to structured meaning:

`SUBSTRATE → (Tri-Unity Engine → Relational Dynamics + Phase Transition) → MSP → MSC → MBC`

MSP provides:
*   The first axes of distinction.
*   The first symmetry classes.
*   The first stable modal structure.
*   The first template for operators.

## 7.2 Definition: What Is a Mode?

A **mode** is a stable symmetry class of relational behavior that emerges after the Substrate's symmetry-breaking. Modes are the earliest possible categories in the system, acting as:
*   A direction of variation.
*   A stable attractor class.
*   A proto-coordinate.
*   A constraint on possible transformations.
*   A foundation for dimensional structure.

Modes are more primitive than concepts, fields, frames, or Boxes, acting as the preconditions for all of these higher-order structures.

## 7.3 The MSP Architecture (Core Components)

MSP is composed of four architectural elements that define its operational capabilities:

### 7.3.1 Modal Axes

Orthogonal (or quasi-orthogonal) directions of possible variation. These are the proto-dimensions that meaning will eventually inhabit.

### 7.3.2 Modal Symmetry Classes

Each symmetry class defines a family of allowed transformations. This is analogous to symmetry groups or coordinate transformations, providing the basis for structured change.

### 7.3.3 Modal Invariants

Every mode has rules that cannot be violated. These invariants form the fundamental "laws of structure" that MSC later enforces for semantic objects. The core MSP invariants are:

*   **M1 — The Invariant of Modal Closure:** All constraints participating in a single resolution must be mutually modal-compatible.
*   **M2 — The Invariant of Symmetry Preservation:** No outcome may be selected if its alternatives are symmetry-equivalent under the current set of constraints.
*   **M3 — The Invariant of Non-Forcing:** The absence of a constraint is not itself a constraint. A `NOT` state cannot be inferred from a lack of evidence for an `IS` state.
*   **M4 — The Invariant of Modal Non-Promotion:** The CRF may not elevate a proposition from a lower modal status to a higher one.
*   **M5 — The Invariant of Reference Neutrality:** The result of an evaluation must be invariant under all symmetry-preserving transformations of its inputs.

### 7.3.4 Proto-Operators (`δ`, `Φ`, `Π`)

For the quantum-level formalization of these operators, including their treatment as quantum operators within a semantic Hilbert space, see [Section 28: Semantic Quantum Theory](28_Semantic_Quantum_Theory.md).

These foundational operators arise naturally within MSP and are the manifestation of the Monistic Tri-Unity (see `manual/26_The_Monistic_Tri-Unity.md`):
*   **`δ` (Deviation):** The ability to detect difference.
*   **`Φ` (Projection):** The ability to map raw signals into modal space.
*   **`Π` (Evaluation):** The ability to test projected forms for consistency.

These are not yet semantic operators, but rather minimal machinery for modal coherence.

## 7.4 MSP as a Coordinate System for Meaning

MSP creates a coordinate system for potential meaning, providing the orientation, axes, degrees of freedom, proto-metrics, and proto-boundaries necessary for any form of meaning to exist. Meaning becomes possible only because MSP first creates a structured space for it.

## 7.5 MSP Modal Partitioning

MSP partitions modal possibility into canonical modal classes that correspond to the 13 Tiers of MBC. These modal classes are the architectural blueprint, while the Tiers are the operational implementations. Examples of these modal classes include deviation, projection, evaluation, adjacency, curvature, oscillation, summation, polarity, semantic-time, global-constraint, meta-structure, universal-schema, and coherence.

## 7.6 MSP → MSC Transition

Once MSP establishes the modal structure (axes, invariants, operators), MSC then introduces the "physics" of this semantic space. MSC governs cohesion, mass, tension, curvature, and propagation limits, turning MSP's abstract modal structure into a dynamic physics of meaning.
